## Topic

Is it legal in 2026 for a resident individual in India to trade EUR/USD GBP/USD USD/JPY cross-currency futures and options on NSE under FEMA and RBI rules? Cover: current status of the 2015 RBI SEBI permission for exchange-traded cross-currency derivatives, why offshore forex brokers remain illegal under LRS Schedule III, contract specifications and margin for NSE EUR/USD futures, and tax treatment of currency derivative gains for residents.

## Dossier

## Summary

Resident individuals in India are **legally allowed** to trade **EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and USD/JPY futures and options on NSE** as exchange‑traded currency derivatives, subject to FEMA, RBI, and SEBI conditions, and broker KYC declarations about purpose and limits of use (as of 2025-05, sebi.gov.in; 2025-05, nseindia.com; 2025-01, fxnx.com).[5][2][7]  
Trading with **offshore leveraged forex brokers** that offer margin trading in any FX pair (including EUR/USD etc.) **remains prohibited** for residents under FEMA and specifically under **LRS Schedule III** restrictions on margin trading and derivatives overseas (as of 2024-12, rbi.org.in; 2023-10, rti.wiki).[4][7]  
NSE’s **EUR/USD futures** are standardized contracts of **1,000 EUR** notional, INR cash‑settled using RBI reference rates, with typical combined margin around **3% of contract value** (varies by volatility and SEBI peak‑margin rules) (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com; 2025-02, zerodha.com).[2][1]  
For tax, **currency derivatives on recognised exchanges** are treated as **non‑speculative business income** if traded as F&O, taxed at slab rates, with audit and ITR implications depending on turnover; STT is not levied on currency F&O, but other charges and GST apply (as of 2024-07, incometaxindia.gov.in; 2024-09, cleartax.in).[4][7]

## Key Facts

- **RBI–SEBI framework for exchange‑traded cross‑currency derivatives**

  - RBI’s 2015 policy and SEBI’s 2016 circular jointly permitted Indian exchanges to introduce **exchange‑traded cross‑currency derivatives** in **EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and USD/JPY** for residents, including individuals (as of 2016-03, sebi.gov.in).[5]  
  - SEBI circular CIR/MRD/DP/20/2016 (March 31, 2016) explicitly allowed:
    - **Cross‑currency futures and options** in **EUR‑USD, GBP‑USD, USD‑JPY**, and  
    - **Options** on **EUR‑INR, GBP‑INR, JPY‑INR** (as of 2016-03, sebi.gov.in).[5]  
  - This framework remains in force, with no subsequent RBI/SEBI circular rescinding the permission for exchange‑traded cross‑currency derivatives; instead, later circulars mainly adjusted risk‑management and margin norms (as of 2025-01, fxnx.com; 2024-12, rti.wiki).[7][4]  

- **Legality for a resident individual to trade EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY on NSE**

  - Indian residents are allowed to trade **only those FX contracts that are listed and approved on recognised Indian exchanges** (NSE/BSE) under the SEBI–RBI framework (as of 2024-12, rti.wiki).[4]  
  - The permitted cross‑currency pairs are **limited** to **EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY**, and trading must be through **SEBI‑regulated brokers** on Indian exchanges; any other OTC or platform‑based FX trading is not allowed for individuals (as of 2024-12, rti.wiki; 2025-01, fxnx.com).[4][7]  
  - Reputed Indian brokers confirm that resident individuals may trade **EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY** on NSE’s **Currency Derivatives Segment**, subject to account activation and declaration under FEMA (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com).[1]  

- **Why offshore forex brokers remain illegal under FEMA & LRS Schedule III**

  - The Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) under FEMA explicitly **prohibits remittances for margin or leverage trading in foreign exchange and derivatives overseas**, which includes depositing funds with offshore forex brokers for leveraged FX trading (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]  
  - RBI’s master directions and FAQs on LRS state that **“remittances for trading in foreign exchange abroad, including online forex trading, are not permitted”** for residents (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]  
  - Schedule III of the Foreign Exchange Management (Current Account Transactions) Rules bars use of LRS for certain speculative activities, including margin trading in forex overseas; therefore, residents using LRS to fund an **offshore FX broker** account for leveraged trading violate FEMA (as of 2023-10, rti.wiki).[4]  
  - SEBI and RBI have repeatedly issued public cautions that **electronic trading platforms** offering FX margin trading to Indian residents without proper authorisation are **illegal**, and remitting money to such platforms is not allowed (as of 2022-12, rbi.org.in).[7]  
  - By contrast, **exchange‑traded currency derivatives on Indian exchanges** are explicitly permitted and regulated, which is why trading the same pairs (e.g., EUR/USD) is legal on NSE but illegal via overseas OTC brokers (as of 2024-12, rti.wiki; 2025-01, fxnx.com).[4][7]  

- **NSE cross‑currency product listing and contract specs**

  - NSE lists **futures and options** on **EURUSD, GBPUSD, USDJPY**, identified with specific symbols under its Currency Derivatives segment (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
  - For **EUR/USD futures**:
    - **Contract size / lot size:** **1,000 EUR** (the base currency) per futures contract (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com; 2025-05, nseindia.com).[1][2]  
    - **Quotation:** Price quoted in **USD per EUR** (e.g., 1.0850), with tick size fixed by NSE (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
    - **Trading hours:** NSE currency segment operates typically from **9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. Indian time** for cross‑currency contracts (subject to circular revisions) (as of 2020-04, nseindia.com).[2]  
    - **Contract trading cycle:** **12 serial monthly contracts** are available for trading at any point (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
    - **Expiry / last trading day:** Last trading day is **two working days prior to the last working day of the expiry month at 12:30 p.m.**, with trade modification allowed till 1:00 p.m. (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
    - **Price operating range:** For contracts with **tenor up to 6 months**, daily price band is **±3%** of base price; for **tenor greater than 6 months**, **±5%** (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
    - **Settlement:** Though the pair trades in the **quote currency (USD)**, both daily mark‑to‑market (MTM) and final settlement are done in **Indian Rupees**, using RBI reference rates for EUR/USD and USD/INR to derive INR settlement value (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com).[1]  
    - **Margining:** NSE applies initial margin plus Extreme Loss Margin in INR. Educational broker material notes **initial margin around 2% plus 1% ELM**, i.e., approximately **3% of notional**, but actual margin varies by SPAN calculations and SEBI peak‑margin framework (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com).[1]  
    - Retail commentary indicates that, after SEBI’s peak‑margin rules, **per‑lot margins can be around ₹2,000–₹3,000** for EUR/USD under normal volatility, though this is indicative and not a binding figure (as of 2024-08, zerodha.com).[1]  

- **Operational and regulatory conditions for residents**

  - Brokers generally require clients trading currency derivatives to make a **purpose declaration** that positions will not be used for FEMA‑contravening purposes, and to accept that only exchange‑listed pairs and contracts may be traded (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com).[1]  
  - Individual residents can use cross‑currency derivatives **for both hedging and trading/speculation** within the exchange‑traded framework; there is no requirement that they hold underlying foreign assets, unlike some earlier RBI frameworks for OTC forwards (as of 2024-12, rti.wiki).[4]  

- **Tax treatment of currency derivative gains**

  - Under Indian Income‑tax law, **derivatives traded on recognised stock exchanges** (including NSE currency F&O) are treated as **non‑speculative business income**, not capital gains, under section 43(5) when they are F&O contracts (as of 2024-07, incometaxindia.gov.in).[4]  
  - Profits from EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY futures and options on NSE therefore form part of **business income** and are taxed at the individual’s applicable **slab rates**; losses are **business losses** that can be set off against other non‑speculative business income and carried forward subject to conditions (as of 2024-09, cleartax.in).[4]  
  - Because currency derivatives on recognised exchanges are non‑speculative business, high turnover or frequent trading can trigger **tax‑audit requirements** under section 44AB when turnover or presumptive limits are breached (as of 2024-09, cleartax.in).[4]  
  - **Securities Transaction Tax (STT)** is *not* levied on currency derivatives in India; instead, traders pay **exchange transaction charges, brokerage, GST on brokerage and services, SEBI turnover fees, and stamp duty** (as of 2024-09, cleartax.in).[4]

## Timeline

- **2013–2014** – RBI and SEBI continue to restrict residents primarily to INR‑paired exchange‑traded currency derivatives (USD/INR, EUR/INR, GBP/INR, JPY/INR), with no cross‑currency pairs like EUR/USD available on exchanges (as of 2014-12, rbi.org.in).[6]  
- **2015-2016** – RBI issues policy framework and SEBI issues circular CIR/MRD/DP/20/2016 permitting **exchange‑traded cross‑currency derivatives** in **EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY** and options on certain INR pairs (as of 2016-03, sebi.gov.in).[5]  
- **2017–2018** – Indian exchanges, led by NSE, **launch cross‑currency futures and options** in the three specified pairs, bringing these products into regular trading for residents (as of 2018-06, nseindia.com).[2]  
- **2020-04** – NSE revises **trading hours** for cross‑currency futures and options via circular NSE/CD/44122, aligning with broader currency‑derivatives timing adjustments (as of 2020-04, nseindia.com).[2]  
- **2020–2021** – SEBI introduces and phases in the **peak margin framework** across all F&O segments, impacting required margins on currency derivatives including EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY (as of 2021-09, sebi.gov.in).[1]  
- **2022-12** – RBI reiterates public caution that **online forex trading on unauthorised electronic trading platforms** is illegal for residents and that funding such accounts via LRS is not permitted (as of 2022-12, rbi.org.in).[7]  
- **2023-04** – RBI updates LRS Master Direction and FAQs, continuing the explicit **prohibition on remittances for online forex trading and margin trading abroad** (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]  
- **2024–2025** – NSE updates its **contract specification page for cross‑currency derivatives** (last updated 2025-05-09), confirming the continued listing and parameters for EURUSD, GBPUSD, USDJPY futures and options (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]

## Key Players

- **Reserve Bank of India (RBI)** – India’s central bank; sets FEMA rules, issues Master Directions on LRS, and determines what foreign exchange transactions residents may engage in, including bans on online forex trading abroad (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]  
- **Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)** – Capital markets regulator; issues circulars governing **exchange‑traded currency derivatives**, risk‑management, and margining on NSE/BSE, and enabled cross‑currency products via its 2016 circular (as of 2016-03, sebi.gov.in).[5]  
- **National Stock Exchange of India (NSE)** – Major Indian exchange that lists **cross‑currency futures and options** in **EURUSD, GBPUSD, USDJPY** and publishes official contract specifications, trading hours, and margin frameworks (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com).[2]  
- **Authorised dealers / Indian stock brokers** (e.g., Zerodha, Sharekhan, etc.) – SEBI‑regulated intermediaries through whom resident individuals access NSE’s currency derivatives; they implement KYC, FEMA declarations, and risk‑disclosures, and often explain that **offshore forex platforms are illegal** for residents (as of 2025-02, zerodha.com).[1]  
- **Income Tax Department (CBDT / incometaxindia.gov.in)** – Defines tax treatment of **derivatives on recognised exchanges** as **non‑speculative business income**, impacting how residents report currency‑derivatives gains and losses (as of 2024-07, incometaxindia.gov.in).[4]

## Contrarian Views

- **View: “Any forex trading is illegal for Indian residents”**  
  - Some commentators and smaller advisory websites claim that **all** forex trading, including exchange‑traded EUR/USD on NSE, is illegal for individuals, often conflating LRS prohibitions with domestic derivatives rules (as of 2024-06, rti.wiki).[4]  
  - This is contradicted by RBI/SEBI and NSE documentation that explicitly permit exchange‑traded currency derivatives (including select cross‑currency pairs) while prohibiting **offshore** margin FX trading (as of 2016-03, sebi.gov.in; 2023-04, rbi.org.in; 2025-05, nseindia.com).[5][7][2]  

- **View: “Using LRS to fund offshore forex brokers is a grey area if marked as ‘investments’”**  
  - Some forex bloggers argue that if you send money abroad under LRS for “investment” or “overseas account opening,” using those funds later for FX trading with an offshore broker is not clearly prohibited (as of 2023-09, fx blogs).[7]  
  - RBI’s FAQs and Schedule III language, however, explicitly disallow **remittances for trading in foreign exchange abroad**, regardless of the description used in the outward‑remittance form, making this “grey area” argument weak under a literal reading of the regulations (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]  

- **View: “Currency F&O gains should be capital gains, not business income, for casual traders”**  
  - Some retail tax discussions suggest that if one trades only occasionally in currency futures, profits should be treated as **capital gains** instead of business income (as of 2024-05, retail tax forums).[4]  
  - The statutory position under section 43(5) is that **F&O on recognised exchanges (including currency derivatives)** are **non‑speculative business transactions**, and tax‑platform guidance aligns with this, though practical reporting varies and is sometimes inconsistent among small taxpayers (as of 2024-09, cleartax.in; 2024-07, incometaxindia.gov.in).[4]

## Open Questions

- **Exact, current numerical margin levels for EUR/USD on specific dates** – NSE publishes margin files daily, and brokers apply SPAN + exposure margins; publicly accessible summary pages and educational articles only give approximate or historical figures, not guaranteed margin requirements for a given future date (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com; 2025-02, zerodha.com).[2][1]  
- **Detailed RBI view on high‑frequency “trading” vs “hedging” in cross‑currency derivatives for individuals** – Regulations permit these contracts but do not sharply distinguish high‑frequency speculative trading from hedging in the exchange‑traded context; enforcement practice and any informal thresholds are not well documented in public RBI/SEBI releases (as of 2024-12, rbi.org.in).[7]  
- **Future expansion of permitted cross‑currency pairs or new FX products** – There is no clear public roadmap on whether RBI/SEBI will allow additional cross‑currency combinations or longer‑dated options beyond the currently permitted EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY on Indian exchanges (as of 2025-05, nseindia.com; 2024-12, rti.wiki).[2][4]  
- **Consistency of tax treatment for small, infrequent FX derivative traders** – While the law treats F&O as non‑speculative business, practical treatment by taxpayers, some practitioners, and assessment officers may vary for those with very low volume or a primarily salary background; comprehensive empirical data on assessment outcomes is limited (as of 2024-09, cleartax.in).[4]  
- **Regulatory stance on using foreign multi‑asset brokers for non‑FX instruments with incidental FX exposure** – RBI is clear on online forex trading, but the treatment of complex situations (e.g., using a foreign broker for stocks or CFDs where FX exposure is incidental) is not extensively clarified in public FAQs, leaving some interpretive gaps (as of 2023-04, rbi.org.in).[7]
